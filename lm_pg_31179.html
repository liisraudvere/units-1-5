Grammar: present perfect

What tense do we use to talk about our experiences?

We have already looked at past simple and past continuous that we use to talk about activities and events that took place in the past.
There is one more very common tense that is often used to talk about our experiences:
  • present perfect, which is used if
    •  we do not care or know when something took place
    • the time of the event or the event itself is still continuing
Present perfect also has a continuous form that is used to emphasise the progressive nature of the activity.

Present perfect
When do we use the present perfect?

The present perfect tense is used very often in English. 
It is made up of two parts: the helping verb HAVE / HAS + III form of the irregular verb / -ED ending of regular verb
HINT! It is very similar in its form and use to the Estonian 'täisminevik' (olen teinud).

We use the present perfect to talk about
  • events that started in the past and continue to the present (I have worked here since 1980)
  • events that have taken place during a time that has not finished yet (my life, today, this year, etc)
  • past events when we want to emphasise the present result (I have lost my helmet = I don't have my helmet at the moment) 
  • past events when the time of the event is not known, mentioned or important (I have seen the movie = It doesn't matter when)
We often use time words such as ever, never, already, yet, just, since, for with present perfect.

How do we know whether to use the present perfect simple or continuous?

We use the present perfect simple
  • if the emphasis is on the result
  • when we want to talk about the number of things done
We use the present perfect continuous
  • if the emphasis is on the progress
  • when we want to talk about the length of time spent

How do we know whether to use the present perfect or past simple?

These two tenses are quite often problematic for students.

The most important rules to remember are:

We use the past simple if we talk about
  • an event completely in the past
    • the event has finished or
    • the time has finished
 We use the present perfect to talk about 
  • an event that is somehow connected to the present
    • the time of the event has not finished or
    • the event itself has not finished
NB! We cannot use the present perfect when we talk about a finished time! AND we cannot use the past simple to talk about an unfinished event or unfinished time!
