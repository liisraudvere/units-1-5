Grammar: articles

What are articles? What are they used for?

There are 3 articles in English - A, AN and THE. 

Articles are used
  • in front of nouns 
    • nouns are words that answer the question "who?" or "what?"
    • if there is an adjective in front of the noun, the article goes before the adjective
  • to show whether we are talking about something specific or something in general
You can jump right to the end of the page to study the review and try the linked exercises, if you feel confident enough.
If you need more help, there are different sections below where you can find more information.
Introduction
What should I know before I start learning about articles?

In order to use articles correctly, you need to understand the difference between  
  • singular and plural nouns (ainsuse ja mitmuse nimisõnad)
    • singular means "one", so a singular noun is something or someone there is only one of - a soldier, an officer
    • plural means "more than one", so plural nouns are things or people there is more than one of - soldiers, officers
      • plural forms are usually made by adding -s to the end of the word
      • for irregular plural forms in English, please look HERE 
  • countable and uncountable nouns (loendatavad ja loendamatud nimisõnad)
    • countable nouns are things or people you can count - one soldier, two soldiers, three soldiers
    • countable nouns have a singular and plural form - an officer, officers
    • uncountable nouns cannot be counted - you CANNOT say "one snow", "one weather"
    • uncountable nouns have no plural form - you CANNOT say "two snows", "two weathers"

A and AN
When do we use A and AN? 

A and AN are used
  • only with singular countable nouns
  • when we talk about something for the first time
  • when we talk about one unspecified thing (we don't know or don't care which one)
A and AN CANNOT be used
  • with uncountable nouns
  • with plural nouns
Please watch the following short video that talks about the rules for using indefinite articles.


Link to the video.


What is the difference between A and AN?

A is used in front of consonant sounds (a boy, a unit, a horse). 
AN is used in front of vowel sounds (an officer, an understanding, an hour).

NB! Note that the difference is not in the letter, but in the sound.
We use AN because A would be difficult to pronounce before vowel sounds.
If there is an adjective before the noun, the article depends on the sound the adjective starts with (an interesting idea; a good idea; an unfogettable dream; a great experience).

THE
When do we use THE? 

We use THE
  • with plural as well as singular nouns
  • with countable as well as uncountable nouns
IF WE BELIEVE THE LISTENER KNOWS WHICH THING WE ARE TALKING ABOUT

because
  • both the speaker and the listener see/hear/etc. the thing (What is the man doing? -> The man we can both see.)
  • the thing has been mentioned before (I bought a car. The car is red.)
  • there is only one of those things (around us)
    • with expressions like 'the first', 'the second', 'the only'
    • with superlative forms (the best, the most interesting), as there can only be one number 1 thing
    • places around us which we assume there is only one of (Where is the bathroom?)
  • we identify the thing, person or place in the same clause/sentence (The man who robbed the bank)
  • certain proper nouns (for more information on using articles with proper nouns look HERE)

How do we know if we should use A/AN or THE?

In very simple terms
  • A/AN is used to talk about one unspecific thing. A/AN means the same as ONE. 
    • I have a car = I have one car. The listener does not know which car I am talking about.
    • There is a shop around the corner = There is one shop. The listener does not know which shop I am talking about.
  • THE is used to talk about a specific thing. If you use THE, you must be able to answer the question 'Which?'.
    • I read the book you recommended. Which book? The one you recommended.
    • Can you open the door? Which door? The door of the room where we both are. 
If we want to talk about unspecific plural things, we use SOME.

Zero article
When don't we use articles before nouns? 

We don't use articles in front of nouns in certain cases:
  • plural nouns when we talk about things in general (I like cats = cats as a species, not these specific cats)
  • abstract/uncountable nouns when we talk about things in general (Life is short. Education is very important.)
  • games and sports (I play football every Monday. I am good at golf.)
  • meals (We had dinner at a restaurant. BUT I had a big breakfast.)
  • noun+number (The train leaves from platfrom 5. I live in apartment 72.)
  • certain nouns if we talk about the main purpose of the place
    • bed (I went to bed early. BUT I am sitting on the bed.)
    • home (I am at home. BUT We bought a new home.)
    • work (I left work early.)
    • school, university, college (Mary goes to school = she studies there. BUT My mom came to the school to talk to the principal.)
    • church (I go to church every Sunday = I participate in the church service. BUT I went inside the church to look at the paintings.)
    • hospital (They took him to hospital = He needed medical attention. BUT We went to the hospital to see Mary.)
    • prison (He is in prison for murder. BUT He volunteers in a prison.)
    • sea (The sailors went to sea. BUT I live by the sea.)
  • movement and transportation (I came on foot. We went there by car.)
  • names
    • proper names (I met Mary. John loves Kate.)
    • countries (I live in Estonia.) EXCEPT 
      • when the name is in plural form (I am from the Neatherlands.) 
      • the type of country is mentioned (I am from the Republic of Estonia.) 
    • cities, towns (I moved to Berlin.)
    • weekdays (He started on Monday.)
    • months (My birthday is in April.)
    • streets (I live on Oxford Street.)
    • languages (I speak Spanish.)
    • academic subjects (I study History.)
There are also special cases for using articles, such as specific expressions and geographical names. You can read about them HERE.
